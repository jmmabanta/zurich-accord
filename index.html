The Zurich Accord

~ A Reworked Treaty of Versailles ~

By: Thomas Jehn, John Marcus Mabanta

Foreword

"This has been a devastating war that has caused nations across the world unimaginable destruction. In the year of our Lord 1918, on the eleventh hour of the eleventh day of the eleventh month, the fighting was ended. Now the Kaiser has fallen and the fate of the German people is in the hands of the Entente. Within the confines of this treaty, the terms of territorial and monetary concessions are outlined for the new German Republic along with restrictions on military and government policy. This treaty aims to create a lasting peace that is not so harsh that the German people turn to radicalism for solutions, but not one without discipline for the German nation."

I. Implementation of a Liberal Democracy

With the ousting of the Kaiser by the German people prior to the armistice which brought the drafting of the Zurich Accord, it is within reason to state that there will not be a return to monarchy. Kaiser Whilhelm II must give up all claims to any territory within the former German Empire, but his direct descendants and any remaining descendants of the House of Hohenzollern may remain as princes, dukes, and any other dynastic title. They may not hold any more political power than local politicians, and they may not hold federal offices. Within a liberal democracy, there is no need to fear the rise of a new monarchy, especially considering the last Kaiser was ousted by the German people, so there is no reason to abolish the House of Hohenzollern as a whole. Dynastic members are only restricted from running for federal offices as a failsafe against monarchism. Forcing an exile would only turn the House of Hohenzollern into a symbol of martyrdom, and therefore pose a threat to a long-lasting peace. The German Empire is, henceforth, formally abolished. The German people and German nation shall now take a liberal democracy of their own creation with a constitution approved by at least two of the three nations listed here: France, Great Britain, and the United States of America. Once the constitution has been approved, no member of the Entente has any right to interfere in German political affairs. Ensuring a stable German Republic is absolutely vital to the stability of Europe, so it shall be a top priority for this treaty.

II. Territorial Concessions

With the Defeat of the Russian Empire on the third of March 1918, the German Empire saw significant territorial and political gains. Many nations were released to become puppets of the Kaiser, including Estonia, Lithuania, Latvia, and Poland, with Finland, Ukraine, and Georgia becoming fully independent. With the defeat of the German Empire, all territories gained from the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk are to be completely relinquished by the German Republic, as the power gained from controlling the majority of Eastern Europe would be detrimental to the balance of power in Europe and would only serve to counteract efforts to create long-lasting peace. Poland shall be granted full political independence, and Southern Silesia shall be relinquished as reparations for the German invasion of ethnically Polish land. Polish merchant ships will have the right to freely use the port of Danzig without fear of interference to promote economic stimulation in the new nation, but the Entente recognizes that there is no right to move the ethnic Germans who live there already, so the city shall remain part of the German Republic. There will be a return of the unlawfully annexed territory of Alsace-Lorraine back to the French Republic. Any and all colonial possessions of the former German Empire shall be ceded to Great Britain and France.

III. War Reparations

There is not a single European nation that has been untouched by war, but some were more impacted than others. Being the primary economic power of the Central Powers, the German Republic will he held economically accountable for the destruction of war. With accountability in mind, the German republic will be forced to pay 75 billion marks in war reparations. 30 billion marks shall go to the French Republic, as the majority of the war was fought on French soil with French lives. 20 billion marks shall go to Great Britain, as it was Britain exerting itself economically and politically the second most. 10 billion marks shall both go to Belgium due to the damages it has suffered as being one of the first contact points in the Western front. 10 billion marks shall also be allotted to Italy as a part of the Treaty of London, a pact to switch alliances to the Entente. Lastly, the United States of America would receive the remaining 5 billion marks for its late, but effective, contributions to the European war effort.

Reparation payments have been significantly reduced compared to the original Treaty of Versailles. This is done to allow Germany's economy to thrive under harsh post-war conditions and to preserve the quality of life of the German people. Moreover, it allows Germany to pay off its war debts in a more reasonable timeframe.

IV. The Rhineland and the Ruhr

The Rhineland and the Ruhr are vital areas for the economy of the German Republic, and it is understood that without access to these areas in their entirety, there is no conceivable way for the German Republic to pay the war reparations assigned. Should reparation payments not be conducted in an orderly timeframe, or if too many payments are missed without first consulting the nations being paid, the Entente has a right to occupy the Rhineland and Ruhr until the economic output seized is sufficient to make up for missed payments. If payments are made in correct quantities regularly, the German Republic is to have full sovereignty over all its territories, and the Rhineland and the Ruhr are no exception.

V. Polish Sea Access

All ports in Prussia are in cities that have been ethnically German for centuries, and it is understood that the tensions that would arise from moving large quantities of ethnic Germans from their homes would be a threat to European stability, so there shall be a compromise made. In accordance with the freedom of the seas outlined as one of Woodrow Wilson's fourteen points, Polish ships shall have free access to German waterways within the baltic, and Polish merchant ships may be constructed and launched from German-controlled ports within East Prussia. The German Republic must allow Polish shipping throughout East Prussia and must allow open borders into East Prussia with the newly founded Poland.

VI. Military Restrictions

The final point of the treaty has to deal with the matter of the German armed forces. The arms race of the early 20th century proved detrimental to the stability of Europe, so there will be restrictions put on any German military activity. The German Republic is to have a standing army of no more than five hundred thousand active servicemen with seven-hundred thousand servicemen in reserve. These numbers encompass support personnel as well. The air force is to be significantly downgraded, and there are to be no more than two-hundred planes in any air force established. The navy may still exist, but there can be no more than fifty ships. Any form of a submarine is strictly prohibited, and any aircraft carrier designs must be first approved by Great Britain before their construction. No German military forces may be stationed in the Rhineland until the war reparations outlined in the Economy section of the treaty have been paid off in full to prevent any military resistance to debt collection. There may be no conscription of troops unless in a defensive war. It is understood that an overly-large German army poses a threat to European stability, but there is an emerging threat in Communist Russia and their expansionist ideological principles. Combating and containing the communist threat could end up being vital to the overall stability of Europe, therefore a somewhat strong German military is a necessity. Of course, the production and possession of chemical weapons would be absolutely forbidden due to its unethical nature as a weapon of mass destruction as evident during its use in the Great War.

Questions:

Q1. What would be challenges faced by peacemakers striving to create a lasting world peace?

The main issue with creating world peace are the conflict of interests between parties and nations. Due to differing national interests, history, and political ideals, the idea of ‘world peace’ differs between nations. For example, France who has a long history of conflict with Germany sees that world peace can only be achieved with, essentially, the complete eradication and humiliation of Germany. As France suffered most of the damage caused by the Germans in the Great War, they see Germany as a threat to a lasting world peace. This is unlike the viewpoint held by the United States as they see world peace requiring the cooperation between all nations. They hold this fairly neutral and ‘pacifist’ approach to world peace not only because of their ‘isolation’ from European historical affairs, but the war also provided the US a great economic opportunity from all sides. To pursue international cooperation between the European nations is to pursue their national economic interests. These conflicts of interests lead to the creation of the Treaty of Versailles, a peace agreement that ultimately failed to create long-lasting world peace. It is a treaty that put full blame on Germany, with clauses that crippled the German economy and outraged the people. It is a treaty that inspired the rise of facism in Germany and Italy, and the coming second global conflict.

Q2. How believable was the German case that they alone were not to blame for WWI?

Germany was one of the key players in starting the Great War, and there is no denying that. However, to assume that the German Empire was acting alone in starting the Great war would be foolish. The balance of power in Europe had been balancing on a knife's edge many times since the German Empire was formally established, and every Eurpoean nation was geared for war. There were so many factors in Europe that lead up to the Great War, and it was almost unfeasible that war could be completely prevented. Nationalisitic sentiments had been growing in every major power in Europe, and even in some minor powers. Weapons technology saw steady advancement since the last large-scale Eurpoean conflict, and nations were overconfident in their military strength. Finally, clashing Imperial ambitions would inevitably come to a head. The world was on the path for war long before the Germans and Austrians made formal alliances. The death of Archduke Ferdinand was just the spark that set off the powder keg of Europe that every nation had a hand in filling. Just about every Eurpoean power was, in some capacity, guilty for the Great War. Britain made no secret about its imperial ambitions, and was constantly at odds with Germany. France had been an aggressor in the Franco-Prussian war about thirty years earlier and was eager for a rematch. Russia was desperate to prove its might after it suffered a humiliating loss to Japan in the Russo-Japanise war. Any Eurpoean nation could have started the Great War by 1914. It just so happened to be one of Germany’s allies that struck the first blow. Another noteworthy element of the Great War is that Germany was just supporting its ally. Austria-Hungary may not have been justified in attacking Serbia, but the fact is that they were the ones who started Europe down the path to war. Germany was just fulfilling their duties as an ally.

Q3. Do you think Germany was treated fairly by the Treaty of Versailles?

The Treaty of Versailles has ultimately failed its goal of maintaining a lasting world peace by its unjust treatment of the German nation-state. In its creation, there were no German representatives giving Germany's side of the story when it came to the war, no German influence on a treaty that would ultimately affect them. Thus, each term of the treaty meticulously targeted every facet of the German nation-state under the eyes of its enemies. Although it is evident that Germany escalated a conflict, that was otherwise a civil dispute among the former Austro-Hungarian empire, the terms that the Treaty of Versailles forced upon Germany were unreasonable and ineffective. Although it was Germany that first used immoral weapons, such as the U-Boat and mustard gas, it cannot be forgotten that the Allies retaliated with the same deadly force. Millions upon millions of innocent have died in the war at the hands of both the Central and Allied powers. The Treaty of Versailles’ ‘war guilt’ clause that it had enforced on Germany unfairly ignores the contributions made by all other parties in perpetuating the World War. Massive war reparations thrashed the German economy, making it almost impossible for the common people to buy a loaf of bread and even for Germany to pay off its ever-increasing war debts. The Zurich Accord aims to relieve Germany of these stresses by loosening up its restrictions and by taking into account its effects on the German people and economy.
